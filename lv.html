LVM in Linux

A graphical representation of Logical Volume Management

1. Physical Volumes (PVs)

These are the **physical storage devices** like hard drives or partitions. LVM uses them as the foundation. Each PV is divided into a number of equally sized **Physical Extents (PEs)**, which are the smallest units of LVM storage.

Hard Disk 1 (/dev/sda1)
Hard Disk 2 (/dev/sdb1)
Hard Disk 3 (/dev/sdc1)

Key takeaway:

PVs are the raw disks or partitions that LVM manages. They are the base layer.

2. Volume Groups (VGs)

A VG is a **pool of storage** created by combining one or more PVs. Think of it as a logical container. The PEs from all the PVs are aggregated into the VG, forming a large pool of free space.

VG: my_data_vg

... a pool of Physical Extents (PEs)

Key takeaway:

VGs are the flexible storage pools you create from your PVs. This is where you allocate space for LVs.

3. Logical Volumes (LVs)

LVs are the **virtual partitions** you can create from the VG's free space. They are the devices you actually use, like `/dev/my_data_vg/media_lv`.

From VG: my_data_vg
LV: /dev/my_vg/home_lv (200G)
LV: /dev/my_vg/web_lv (50G)
LV: /dev/my_vg/db_lv (10G)

Key takeaway:

LVs are the flexible partitions that you can resize on the fly. They are the final layer you interact with.

This diagram illustrates how LVM creates an abstraction layer. You start with physical disks, combine them into a single logical pool (the Volume Group), and then carve out flexible partitions (the Logical Volumes) from that pool. This decouples the file system from the physical hardware, allowing for easy resizing, snapshotting, and migration.
